Design Approach

How I Design

My approach to instructional design comes from ten years of building courses, producing educational media, and studying how people actually learn — not in the abstract, but in specific contexts with specific constraints. Three principles run through everything I make:

Structure as Scaffolding

Students learn better when they aren't spending cognitive effort figuring out how a course works. I design with consistent, predictable architectures — the same module flow each week, the same assignment rhythm, the same escalating logic from context to theory to application. Predictability isn't rigidity; it's what frees students to focus on the material instead of the logistics.

Critical Engagement over Coverage

I design courses that prioritize depth of analysis over breadth of content. The goal is developing transferable skills — evaluating evidence, constructing arguments, identifying assumptions — not marching through a textbook. My courses culminate in projects where students apply analytical frameworks to subjects they actually care about, which consistently produces stronger writing and more genuine intellectual engagement.

Inclusive by Design

As a first-generation college student, I know what it's like to navigate institutions that weren't built with you in mind. That experience shapes concrete design choices: asynchronous delivery and narrated lectures for students with unreliable internet or inflexible schedules; capped weekly workloads to keep cognitive load on the content rather than the course structure; alternative assessment pathways when a single format creates barriers unrelated to the learning objective; and rubrics distributed in advance so evaluation criteria aren't a mystery.
